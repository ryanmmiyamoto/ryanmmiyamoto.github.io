Overview


My tenure at the Spondylitis Association of America was a culmination of my desire to use my computer science knowledge in a way that could help individuals and families. Prior to my arrival, SAA was outsourcing all of their web-related responsibilities to a web solutions company. I took complete control of their website, assuming all duties saving SAA over 30% of their annual expenses in the process.

This required a ton of new learning as the transition forced me to learn an entirely new CMS and CRM software as well as figuring out how to integrate custom modules and plugins. Other responsibilities included: SEO optimization, graphic design using Adobe products, eCommerce management and gamification implementation.


Notable Achievements


Along with the main SAA website, I also assumed control of their child site, which was dedicated to providing information for kids & teens inflicted with the disease. The site was old and outdated so I migrated and redesigned their entire website to WordPress. This not only greatly improved the look and feel, but increased user-friendliness, which allowed coworkers with less technical knowledge to make content changes.

In May 2019, we hosted a Global Summit - a 3-day online event where users registered in advance and were granted access to exclusive presentations by leaders in medical research. I was responsible for creating and designing the platform used to host the presentations on our website - including the registration system, video hosting solution, Q&A module, and role-based security system.

What did I learn?


Empathy. Working for a non-profit that services those afflicted by disease can be an eye-opening experience. The gratitude our users would show, combined with the experience they would share with us, really made me focus on putting out the highest quality work possible.

Leadership. Being the sole engineer on-site made me the foremost authority on all things web-related. All design decisions ultimately fell under my jurisdiction. As a result, I learned how to teach coworkers to perform basic CMS functions as well as properly explain any compromises to less-technical staff.
